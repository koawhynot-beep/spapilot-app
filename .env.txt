Everyone talks about "the cloud" like all your photos your messages your spotify songs are stored and floating up in the sky, right? Yeah thats a lie. The internet isnt in a cloud or "the cloud". its not even in the air. Around 95% of all internet traffic actually travels through cables sitting on the ocean floor, sometimes 3 miles deep. One of these cables actually got bitten by a shark in 2014, but ill cover that later. anyways right nwo there is around 600 active cables crossing the worlds oceans. the total length, if you layed them end to end comes to around 900 thousand miles which is enough to wrap around the earth 37 times.



 Now, when someone pictures a cable underwater, they might imagine a thick metal pipe, but its not. its litterally about as wide as a garden hose, and the part that carries the internet is made of glass fibers thinner than a human hair, that tiny strand is whats connecting entire nations, and the way they shoot internet through the cables is kind of insane, its basically firing lasers down a glass tube. Heres how it works. on one end of the cable, lasers fire pulses of light through the hair thin glass fibers, on the other end, receptors catch the light then convert it into data.



 the light travels two thirds of the speed of light, which can cross an ocean in less than a second. But  the light loses strength over distance, think of it like shining a flashlight down a really long hallway. the light still moves at the same speed no matter how far the hallway is. but the further it goes, the dimmer it gets at the other end. some of the light gets absorbed by the glass fibers itself and by the time it travels 60 to 100 kilometers, the laser pulse is so dim the end of the cable can't read it cleanly anymore. so every 60 to 100 kilometers theres something built into the cable that boosts the signal back up.  And this whole setup is exactly why satellites physically cannot replace these cables. Let me prove that right now.



  One single cable called MAREA, which Microsoft and Meta laid in 2018 between the U.S. and Spain, can transmit 224 terabits of data per second. One terabit is fast enough to send roughly 12 full 4K movies every second. So a single cable can handle millions of people streaming, FaceTiming, and gaming all at the same time without breaking a sweat. Now keep that number, 224, locked in your head. Because when I compare it to Starlink in a minute, you're going to laugh.



every single one of elon musk's starlink satellites combined has a total capacity of around 24 terabits per second while a single one of the cables microsoft laid is 224 terabits per second. By the way if you dont know what terabits per second is it's a measurement of how fast data gets transferred. 



So a single cable beats the entire Starlink fleet by almost ten times. On top of that, a $300 million underwater cable lasts 25 years or more while a starlink satellite costs way more, dies in about 10 years, and the whole network of starlink costs billions to maintain.



Starlink is amazing for cruise ships, remote islands, basically places cables can't reach. But it's not replacing the backbone of the internet. So if the underwater cables are this important, what   happens when one of them breaks? Well it happens way more than you think. On average 150-200 cables break every year. But what's the cause?  



About 80% of all cable damage comes from ship anchors and fishing boats dragging across the seabed and ripping the cables apart. Around 7% comes from natural events, things like earthquakes, underwater landslides, volcanic eruptions while the rest of the damage is simply caused by wear and tear, equipment failure, and rare cases of sabotage.  



And yes, in 2014 a shark actually did bite a google cable. There's literally video footage. A robot was just down there doing its job recording the entire seafloor as it moved along and a shark just happened to swim up and bite the cable while the camera was pointing at it. after that Google ended up adding kevlar to their cables which is the same stuff that bulletproof vests use. But here's where it gets crazy, like i said Some of these breaks arent accidents but sabotaged.



in 2014, an Islamic political and military organization called houthi damaged multiple internet cables in the red sea and that single incident disrupted around 25% of all internet traffic between europe and asia. internet speeds in india, pakistan, the UAE and Kuwait dropped overnight, And Russia and China have both ALLEGEDLY been mapping cable routes for years.



Here's the scary part. The only thing legally protecting these cables is an 1884 Paris Convention and the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea. There's no army guarding the cables. They're basically just garden hoses sitting in international waters carrying 99% of the worlds internet which is something people value and rely on ALOT. So who actually owns this stuff? Meta, Google, Microsoft, and Amazon together control over 70% of the world's cables, it used to be the big telecom companies that owned everything but now its four tech giants
